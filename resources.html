Resources & Methodology

This page explains where the data comes from, what the taxonomy actually measures, and, just as important, what this site does not claim.

How to read these scores

Every video gets an overall score and a breakdown across three categories: Pacing Intensification, Recovery Denial, and Reward Patterning. Each category is built from named, computationally-measured features, never a subjective judgment call.

  • Good calmer than most of the dataset
  • Moderate typical for this dataset
  • Extremely High substantially more intense than most

The Taxonomy: 3 Categories, 10 Pattern Types, 13 Features

Pacing Intensification

How fast the video moves visually.

  • Rapid Cutting: how often the shot changes.
  • Scene Discontinuity: how visually different one shot is from the next.
  • Chromatic Instability: how much the color palette shifts frame to frame.
  • Visual Intensity: overall color saturation and vividness.
  • Continuous Visual Motion: how much of the video has visible motion, and how rarely it holds still.

Recovery Denial

How rarely the video lets a viewer's arousal come back down.

  • Sustained Audio Intensity: average loudness across the video.
  • Loudness Oscillation: how often volume jumps sharply.
  • Silence Elimination: what share of the video is near-total silence.

Reward Patterning

How often the video sets up a small payoff.

  • Musical Build-Resolve: how often music builds tension and releases it.
  • Surprise Reveals: how often a cut lines up with a sudden sound burst.

A supplementary, exploratory signal set derived from video transcripts/subtitles (obligation language, parasocial phrasing, cliffhanger cues) may appear labeled separately in the raw data. It is not part of the core, content-independent taxonomy above and is not used in the headline scores on this site.

At a Glance

Dataset Scope

This site reflects videos scored so far by an ongoing research pipeline, not necessarily its full eventual scope. Exact, live counts are shown below.

The dataset is produced by a separate measurement pipeline (ffmpeg/OpenCV feature extraction) and joined/percentile-ranked by scripts/build_dataset.py. This site does not analyze video files itself and cannot score a video the pipeline hasn't already measured. See the Lookup page for what happens when a search doesn't match.

What This Site Is Not

  • Not a live video analysis tool. It cannot process a video the pipeline hasn't already measured.
  • Not a parental-control or content-filtering product. It doesn't block or rate content appropriateness.
  • Not a diagnostic or clinical tool. It makes no claims about individual developmental outcomes.
  • Not a claim of broader coverage than the data actually has. If the dataset is a pilot/subset, that's stated wherever counts or scores appear.

Common Questions

Does a high score mean this video is bad for my kid?

Not on its own. A high score means the video is produced in a more intense style than most of this dataset: fast cuts, sustained loud audio, frequent reward payoffs. This site measures production style only; it doesn't judge educational value, age appropriateness, or make any claim about developmental harm.

What does "percentile" actually mean here?

A percentile of 73 means this video scores higher on that metric than 73% of the videos currently in the dataset. It's a relative ranking within this specific dataset, not a fixed or universal scale. A score can shift slightly as more videos are added.

Why isn't my favorite video/channel in the dataset?

The dataset only includes videos the research pipeline has specifically processed. It doesn't cover all of YouTube, and coverage is still growing. See "Dataset Scope" above for the current count.

Why does a video show "Not enough data" for some categories?

Some videos have only been partially processed by the pipeline so far. A type shows "Not enough data" only for the specific metrics that haven't been measured yet, not the whole video. Everything that has been measured is still a real score.

Who built this and can I trust the methodology?

Every score comes from named, computationally-measured features (cut rate, loudness, color shifts, and so on), never a subjective judgment call. The full taxonomy and feature list are documented above, and the data pipeline (scripts/build_dataset.py) is openly available in the project repository.
